# Blog Post: The Speakeasy Demo — Creating Space for Engineers to Go Deep

By Max Ellis | April 2026

## Key Takeaways
- Formal demos optimize for visibility; informal ones optimize for learning
- Engineers share more when there's no time pressure or management audience
- Small, organic initiatives can meaningfully change engineering culture

## Content

Most engineering orgs have weekly tech demos. At Max's company, each engineer gets five minutes, there's an audience, and the format rewards polish over depth. It's great for visibility but five minutes is a constraint — you can show what you built but can't explain how or why.

### The Conversation Over Drinks

The idea started at a bar after work. A coworker mentioned something he wanted to share with the team, but it wasn't the kind of thing that fit into a five-minute slot. A lot of engineers on the team are siloed. Juniors don't have many opportunities for mentorship or deep technical conversations. Many engineers are anxious about presenting in the formal demo format.

Max pitched the idea: a monthly "speakeasy" demo. After work, last Friday of the month, no time limits, no management, no pressure. Just engineers going deep on whatever they want to talk about.

### Setting It Up

Max created a Discord server and invited engineers from different teams, deliberate about cross-team representation to break down silos. Within a few days, nine engineers and a QA joined. Monthly cadence was chosen intentionally — frequent enough to build momentum, infrequent enough that it doesn't feel like an obligation.

### The First Session

Five engineers showed up: two seniors, two juniors, and a mid-level. Max went first, demoing the visitor resolution system for the AI product — twenty minutes going deep on Kafka topics, cookie ID comparison, and how the agent works. The juniors asked questions they never would have asked in the formal setting.

Another senior engineer took forty minutes walking through how OpenFlow works, then branched into Kafka internals. Since Max originally set up OpenFlow for the org, there was a lot of back-and-forth. A third demo wasn't even engineering-related — a RAID storage system built at home.

### What People Said After

The senior engineer said it was a much better environment for knowledge sharing. A junior engineer texted Max afterward: "I love that you've brought a cluster of coworkers together. It seems to be helping all of us remember that we work with humans."

### Keeping It Organic

A principal QA and staff engineer have expressed interest in joining. Max is letting it grow naturally. He's intentional about keeping management out — not because there's anything to hide, but because the value is that engineers can be candid, ask "dumb" questions, and share things that aren't polished enough for a formal audience.

### Why This Matters to Max

The through-line of his career is bringing people together and helping others grow — from wildland firefighting (crew cohesion), to STEM tutoring (meeting people where they are), to engineering (making sure people understand the systems).

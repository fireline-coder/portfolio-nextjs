# Blog Post: Teaching Made Me a Better Engineer (and Vice Versa)

By Max Ellis | April 2026

## Key Takeaways
- The best debugging skill is understanding how someone else thinks — whether they're a student or a distributed system
- Meeting people where they are matters more than knowing the answer
- The Socratic method works just as well in code review as it does in a tutoring session

## Content

Before becoming a software engineer, Max was a STEM tutor from 2015 to 2019 at Clark College. He tutored every math class from elementary algebra through Calculus IV, differential equations, and linear algebra. Every computer science class. Every electrical engineering class. Physics. Some chemistry. He also started tutoring classes he hadn't taken — business law, nursing, mechanical engineering. He found the skill wasn't knowing the answer. It was knowing how to get there with someone.

### The Student Who Changed How Max Teaches

There's a student Max will never forget — an older guy with a welding background. He knew some fractions from his trade but didn't believe one-seventh was a real fraction. Instead of explaining why he was wrong, Max tried something different: changing his relationship with the material first. They did fraction work and it started clicking. He asked for something harder. As he left, he told the front desk Max needed a raise.

The lesson: the first job isn't teaching the material. It's changing the relationship with the material. If someone has decided they can't do something, no amount of explanation helps until you address that belief first.

### Meeting People Where They Are

Everyone learns differently. Some students need to hear an explanation. Others need to see it drawn out. Part of the job is figuring that out quickly.

Max does the same with junior engineers. When someone is stuck on a CORS error, a unit test, or Terraform, he first figures out how they learn. If they're auditory, he explains it. If they're visual, he draws it. Then he has them explain it back — because being able to explain something is the real test of understanding.

### The Socratic Method in Code Review

The reason Max could tutor classes he'd never taken is the Socratic method. Instead of giving answers, he asks questions and learns alongside the student. He uses the same approach in engineering: instead of saying "this won't work because of X," he'll ask "what happens when this gets called concurrently?" The engineer arrives at the issue themselves. It's slower in the moment, but it scales.

### Debugging Mental Models

Debugging a student's understanding and debugging a distributed system use the same core skill. In both cases, you're building a mental model of something you can't directly observe. When a student gets the wrong answer, the interesting question is "where did their reasoning diverge from reality?" Production bugs work the same way — the real work is figuring out where the system's behavior diverged from what you expected.

### What Engineering Taught Max About Teaching

Engineering taught him to think in systems. When a student struggles, instead of focusing on the immediate problem, he now thinks about the system: What prerequisite knowledge is shaky? What's their study process? It also taught him to value feedback loops — in engineering you write tests to catch regressions; in tutoring, having a student explain something back is the test.

### The Thread That Connects It All

The through-line of Max's career isn't a technology or a domain. It's helping people understand things — whether that's a student who doesn't believe in fractions, a junior engineer debugging their first CORS error, or a group of engineers in a Discord server going deep on how Kafka works. The tools change. The skill is the same: figure out where someone is, meet them there, and help them get to where they need to be.
